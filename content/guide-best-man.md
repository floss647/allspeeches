# How to Write a Best Man Speech

*By Adrian Simpson, professional speechwriter · Updated [Month Year]*

Being asked to be best man is a real honour — right up until you remember the speech. The good news: a great best man speech isn't about being a natural comedian. It's about structure, a few well-told stories, and knowing the rules. This guide walks you through all of it, with examples you can borrow and adapt. I've written hundreds of these professionally, and everything I know is below.

## How long should a best man speech be?
**A best man speech should last around five minutes — roughly 650 to 800 words spoken aloud. Anything over seven minutes and you'll feel the room start to drift.**

Five minutes is the sweet spot: long enough to tell two or three proper stories, short enough that you leave them wanting more. Time yourself reading it out loud (out loud always runs slower than in your head). If you're over eight minutes, cut — it's almost always the second-best story that needs to go, not the best one.

## How do you start a best man speech?
**Open with a short, confident line that gets a laugh or grabs attention in the first ten seconds — then introduce yourself. Don't open with "For those who don't know me…" — it's the flattest start there is.**

Your opening has one job: to relax the room (and you). A little self-deprecation works brilliantly, because the audience is quietly nervous on your behalf and a laugh tells them they're in safe hands.

A few opening styles that work:
- **The disclaimer:** "Good evening, everyone. For those who don't know me, I'm Tom, the best man — and for those who do know me, I'm sorry, and I promise to keep this brief and only mildly humiliating."
- **The fake nerves:** "They say public speaking is most people's number one fear, with death coming in second. So if anyone needs me, I'll be lying down quietly in that coffin at the back."
- **The set-up and turn:** "When James asked me to be his best man, I was genuinely touched. Then I realised he'd asked four other people first."

Whatever you choose, get a laugh early. It buys you the room for everything that follows.

## What should a best man speech include?
**A classic best man speech follows a simple arc: opening hook, introduce yourself, how you know the groom, a couple of stories about him, a warm word about the couple, then the toast.**

Here's the structure I use for almost every best man speech:

1. **Opening hook** — your laugh in the first ten seconds.
2. **Introduce yourself** — who you are, how you know the groom.
3. **The groom, before** — what he was like when you met him; the affectionate mickey-taking.
4. **One or two real stories** — the heart of the speech. Specific, vivid, and building to a point.
5. **The turn** — how meeting their partner changed him for the better.
6. **A sincere word about the couple** — drop the jokes for a moment and mean it.
7. **The toast** — short, clear, glasses up.

The shape matters: laughs first, warmth second, toast last. You're taking the room from laughter to a lump in the throat in five minutes.

## How do you tell jokes without offending anyone?
**Aim every joke at the groom, never at the bride, the families, or anything that genuinely embarrasses someone. The test: would the groom laugh, and would his nan be fine hearing it? If not, cut it.**

The funniest best man speeches are warm, not cruel. You're roasting a friend you love, in front of people who love him too. Practical rules:
- **Punch sideways and up, never down.** Tease the groom's terrible dancing, not anyone's appearance, exes, or finances.
- **Keep it clean enough for grandparents.** Assume the youngest and oldest people in the room are both listening — because they are.
- **No ex-partners. Ever.** It's the single most common way to kill a room.
- **Specific beats crude.** A true, well-observed detail ("he ironed his jeans for the first date") always lands better than a rude generic gag.
- **Read it to someone honest** before the day. If they wince, the room will too.

## What are some best man speech examples?
**Below is a short worked example you can adapt. Keep the structure; swap in your own names and stories.**

> "Good evening, everyone. For those who don't know me, I'm Tom — James's best man. For those who do know me, yes, he really couldn't find anyone better, and believe me, he looked.
>
> James and I met at university, in halls, eighteen years ago. I knew we'd be friends the moment I saw him attempt to cook pasta by putting it in the kettle. He's grown a lot since then. He now owns a saucepan.
>
> Back then, James was — how do I put this kindly — a work in progress. The man colour-coded his sock drawer but couldn't find his own front door after a night out. He once spent forty minutes looking for the phone he was talking on.
>
> And then he met Sarah. I remember him calling me the next morning, and I knew straight away something was different — he sounded happy in a way I hadn't heard before. Sarah, you took my chaotic, kettle-pasta-making friend and turned him into a man who books restaurants in advance and owns a matching set of towels. That is no small miracle, and we are all grateful.
>
> But here's the real thing. James is the most loyal friend a person could have — the one who turns up, who remembers, who quietly does the right thing when no one's watching. Sarah, you've married one of the good ones. James, you've married someone even better than you deserve, and you know it.
>
> So please, everyone, raise your glasses. To James and Sarah — to love, laughter and happily ever after. The bride and groom."

That's around 280 words — for a five-minute speech, you'd add one more story in the middle. Notice the shape: laugh, affection, turn, sincerity, toast.

## How do you end a best man speech (the toast)?
**End by dropping the jokes, saying one genuine thing about the couple, then inviting everyone to raise a glass with a clear, simple toast.**

The toast is your landing. Keep it short and unmistakable so the room knows exactly when to lift their glasses. A reliable formula:
- One warm, sincere sentence about the couple.
- "Please raise your glasses…"
- "To [the couple] / the bride and groom."

Pause before the toast. Let the warm bit breathe. Then lift your glass clearly so everyone follows.

## What mistakes should you avoid?
**The big ones: going too long, in-jokes nobody else gets, mentioning exes, reading without practising, and drinking too much beforehand.**

- **Too long.** Five minutes. Discipline beats indulgence.
- **In-jokes.** If only three people get it, cut it — the other ninety-seven are watching three people laugh.
- **Winging it.** Practise out loud at least five times. Know it well enough to look up.
- **Dutch courage.** A little to settle the nerves is fine; three pints is a memory you'll regret.
- **Forgetting the cards.** Print it large, number the pages, and have a backup on your phone.
- **No story.** A list of adjectives ("James is funny, kind and generous") is forgettable. One specific story does more than ten adjectives.

## Your best man speech checklist
- [ ] Under five minutes, timed out loud
- [ ] A laugh in the first ten seconds
- [ ] One or two specific, true stories
- [ ] Affectionate, never cruel — nan-proof
- [ ] No exes, no genuine embarrassment
- [ ] A sincere word about the couple before the toast
- [ ] A short, clear toast
- [ ] Practised aloud five times, printed large, backup on phone
