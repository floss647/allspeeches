# How to Write a Father of the Bride Speech

*By Adrian Simpson, professional speechwriter · Updated [Month Year]*

Traditionally the first speech of the day, the father of the bride sets the tone: proud, warm, a little funny, and genuinely moving. It's a wonderful thing to give well — and entirely doable with the right structure. Here's how, with examples.

## How long should a father of the bride speech be?
**A father of the bride speech should last around five minutes — roughly 650 to 800 words. As the opener, it's better a little short than a little long.**

You're warming the room up for the speeches to follow, so keep it tight and generous. Five minutes lets you tell one lovely story about your daughter, welcome her partner, and toast the couple without overstaying.

## How do you start a father of the bride speech?
**Open by welcoming everyone warmly on behalf of yourself and your partner, then add one light, affectionate line to settle the room. As the first speaker, a gracious welcome is expected of you.**

For example: "On behalf of my wife and I, welcome — thank you all for being here to celebrate our daughter. I've been told I have two jobs today: to keep this short, and not to cry. We'll see how both go."

A gentle joke about your own emotion is perfect here — it's honest, and it gives everyone permission to feel something too.

## What should you say about your daughter?
**Tell one specific, affectionate story that shows who she is — from childhood to the woman she's become. A single vivid memory does far more than a list of qualities.**

This is the heart of the speech. Pick a memory that captures her character: the determined five-year-old, the kind teenager, the moment you realised she'd grown into herself. Then bring it to today — how proud you are of who she is. Specifics and warmth, not a CV of achievements.

## How do you welcome the groom and his family?
**Welcome your daughter's partner sincerely into the family, say one genuine thing you admire about them, and acknowledge their parents. This is one of the most important jobs of the speech.**

A warm welcome to your new son- or daughter-in-law — and a nod to their family — brings everyone together and is always well received. Mention something real: how they treat your daughter, how they've fitted in, how glad you are to gain them.

## How do you handle emotion?
**Acknowledge the emotion rather than fighting it, slow down, and have water nearby. A pause or a wobble doesn't spoil the speech — the room is entirely on your side.**

Everyone expects a father of the bride to be moved, and they love him for it. Practise the most emotional lines out loud many times so they're familiar; slow right down when you reach them; pause if you need to. A moment of genuine feeling is the speech working, not failing.

## Father of the bride speech examples
**Here's a short worked passage to adapt.**

> "On behalf of my wife and I, thank you all for being here. I've been dreading and looking forward to today in roughly equal measure.
>
> When Emily was small, she used to stand on my feet so we could dance together in the kitchen. She was absolutely certain she was leading. Some things, I'm pleased to say, never change.
>
> Watching her grow into the kind, funny, fiercely determined woman she is today has been the great privilege of my life. And today she's leading again — walking towards exactly the life she wants.
>
> James, from the day we met you, it was clear how much you love her, and how happy you make her. Welcome to the family — we couldn't have chosen better ourselves.
>
> Ladies and gentlemen, please raise your glasses. To the bride and groom — to Emily and James."

The shape: welcome, one specific memory, who she's become, welcome the groom, toast.

## Mistakes to avoid
**Avoid embarrassing your daughter, listing achievements like a CV, forgetting to welcome the groom's side, and going on too long as the opener.**

- **Embarrassing stories.** Affectionate, never mortifying — she has to sit there.
- **The CV.** Character and warmth beat grades and job titles.
- **Forgetting the in-laws.** Always welcome the partner and acknowledge their family.
- **Overrunning.** You're the warm-up; keep it tight.
- **Not practising the emotional lines.** Familiarity is what gets you through them.
