# How to Write a Groom Speech

*By Adrian Simpson, professional speechwriter · Updated [Month Year]*

The groom's speech does more work than any other: it thanks everyone, raises a few laughs, and says the thing about your partner that the whole room is waiting for. Get the structure right and it almost writes itself. This guide shows you how, with examples you can adapt.

## How long should a groom speech be?
**A groom speech should run around five to seven minutes — roughly 700 to 950 words — because it carries more thank-yous than the others. Keep it under eight.**

You have more to fit in, but the rule still holds: leave them wanting more. Group your thank-yous so they don't become a roll-call, and protect time for the part everyone actually wants — what you say about your partner.

## Who should the groom thank, and in what order?
**Thank, in roughly this order: the guests for coming, both sets of parents, anyone who helped with the wedding, the wedding party, and finally — and most warmly — your partner.**

A simple order that flows:
1. Everyone, for being there.
2. Your partner's parents (and a warm word about being welcomed into the family).
3. Your own parents.
4. Anyone who helped make the day happen.
5. The best man, ushers, bridesmaids, maid of honour.
6. Your partner — saved for last, given the most time and the most heart.

Weave thanks into a sentence of warmth rather than a flat list, and the whole section lifts.

## How do you start a groom speech?
**Open with a warm, slightly funny line — often a thank-you with a twist — then settle into the speech. As groom, you can be a touch more sincere than the best man right from the start.**

For example: "Well — my wife and I…" (pause for the cheer) "…I've been practising that line all week and I still can't quite believe it." Opening on "my wife/husband" is a classic for a reason: it gets an instant, warm reaction.

## What should you say about your partner?
**Say something specific and true — a small, real detail about why you love them — rather than general praise. Specifics move people; superlatives don't.**

Avoid "she's beautiful, kind and amazing." Reach for the particular: the thing they do that you noticed early, the moment you knew, the way they are when no one's watching. One honest, specific sentence about your partner will be the line people remember from the whole wedding.

## How do you balance funny and heartfelt?
**Lead with the lighter material — the thank-yous and a couple of gentle laughs — and build steadily towards sincerity, finishing on the heartfelt note about your partner.**

The emotional shape is a rising one. Laughs settle your nerves and warm the room early; the sincerity lands hardest at the end, once you've earned it. Don't bounce back to a joke after the big heartfelt moment — finish on the feeling.

## Groom speech examples
**Here's a short worked passage you can adapt — swap in your own names and details.**

> "On behalf of my wife and I — and yes, I'm going to say that as often as I can get away with today — thank you all for being here. It means the world to have everyone we love in one room.
>
> To Sarah's mum and dad: thank you for welcoming me so completely, and for raising the person who makes me better every single day. To my own parents: thank you for everything — I hope I've made you proud.
>
> And to Sarah. People kept asking me this week if I was nervous. I'm not. From the moment you laughed at my terrible joke on our first date — out of pity, you've since admitted — I knew. You are the kindest, funniest, most genuine person I've ever met, and somehow you said yes. I'll spend the rest of my life making sure you don't regret it.
>
> Ladies and gentlemen, please raise your glasses — to my wife. To Sarah."

Notice the order: thanks, a small specific detail ("laughed at my terrible joke… out of pity"), then the sincere finish and toast.

## Common mistakes to avoid
**Avoid the never-ending thank-you list, forgetting to mention your partner's family, inside jokes, and saving no time for the part that matters most.**

- **The roll-call.** Group and warm up your thanks; don't read a register.
- **Reading it cold.** Practise out loud — emotion is harder to deliver than you expect.
- **All jokes, no heart** (or all heart, no relief). You need both.
- **Forgetting the new in-laws.** A warm word about being welcomed in goes a long way.
- **Rushing the partner bit.** It's the whole point. Give it room.

## Your groom speech checklist
- [ ] Five to seven minutes, timed aloud
- [ ] Thank-yous grouped and warm, not a list
- [ ] Both families acknowledged
- [ ] One specific, true thing about your partner
- [ ] Builds from light to heartfelt
- [ ] Clear toast to your partner at the end
- [ ] Practised aloud, printed large
